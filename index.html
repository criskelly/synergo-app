Sequencing Tips

Home
Well planned and properly sequenced activities are critical to achieving good outcomes and can help to reduce the risk of accidents. Here are some ideas and things to consider when sequencing activities:

Assess the group: What are they ready for? What do they need?

Remember the program purpose and goals. Why are they there? What do they want to get out of the experience?

Think about your Desired Learned Outcomes for each activity: what is the purpose of the activity? What do you want them to get out it? How does it fit the overall goal of the day?

Match the skills of the group with the challenge you give them to avoid boredom or frustration

The flow of the experience should have a beginning, middle, and end: Activities should build on each other towards a crest and then come back down towards a conclusion

Gaming Tips

Home

Remember the point is to have fun.

It is easy to get caught in worries about the experience, especially when weather or other unforeseen problems enter the picture. As long as people are having fun, and you are able to adapt to the problems, people will continue to learn. Know what you can do to make an experience fun and do it.

Your leadership can make an experience better (or worse).

Activities are not all that important; it’s what you do with the activity that is important. A good leader can usually make a bad game work, but a poor leader can kill the best of games.

Plan more activities than you need.

Having more activities than you could possibly need helps inspire confidence. You need to remember, however, that the sequence of games is important; if you spend all day on icebreakers the learning is diminished. You need to be flexible and insightful in giving the group what they need.

The first 15 minutes set the tone for the experience.

The first few minutes set the tone for the whole experience. Use your best games and your sure-fire experiences to begin a session.

Facilitate, don’t recreate.

Good debriefs can turn bummer experiences into powerful tools for learning. Fun for fun’s sake is okay, but remember that fun is the doorway to learning.

Birdie on A Perch

Home
  • Birdie on A PerchAdd

Guidelines

Participants get into pairs and figure out who will be the “birdie” and who will be the “perch.” Perches make a circle and birdies make a circle around them. Both groups begin circling in opposite directions. When the facilitator calls “Birdie on a Perch,” the birdies must find their perch as quickly as possible and get off the ground without hurting themselves or others. Possible methods include piggyback, sitting on your perch’s knee, standing on your perch’s feet, and jumping into your perch’s arms. The last pair to get their birdie of the ground leaves the circles and becomes the “Calling Birds” who will now call out “Birdie on a Perch.” Continue until one pair is left.

Variations

  • Have birdies make bird noises and motions as they fly around the circle
  • Scatter spots on the ground as another more comfortable option for birdies – the birdie and perch must share a spot if they choose this option

Equipment

  • None (unless using spots variation)

Minefield

Home
  • MinefieldAdd

Guidelines

Create a mine field with boundary markers and scatter plenty of soft objects within it. Participants are in pairs, with one of them blindfolded. The goal is for the sighted partner to verbally direct the blindfolded partner through the mine field without touching any of the mines. Only blindfolded partners can enter the mine field. If they touch mines, they must start over. When the first set of partners make it through, have the partners switch roles.

Variations

  • Designate objects (distinguishable from mines) that the partners need to retrieve before they exit the mine field
  • When people have to start over, rearrange the mines

Equipment

  • Boundary markers (ropes, cones, etc.), plenty of mines (soft throwables, carpet squares, boppers/noodles, etc.), blindfolds for half of the group

Desired Learned Outcomes

  • Communication
  • Trust Building

Reflection Questions

  1. What made communication difficult?
  2. How did you overcome the communication barrier? What did your partner do that you appreciated?
  3. Where else do we have communication barriers in everyday life?

Giants, Wizards, and Elves

Home
  • Giants, Wizards, and ElvesAdd

Guidelines

Split the group up into two teams. Make boundaries that form a rectangle. Establish a line in the center of the rectangle and lines at the back of each side to illustrate each team’s safe zone. To start the game off each team is to huddle together and decide whether they are going to be Giants, Elves, or Wizards. Giants beat Elves, Elves beat Wizards, and Wizards beat Giants. Demonstrate the gestures:

Giants - hands above head with arms straight

Elves - squat down with fingers by the head to make ears

Wizards - arms out like casting a spell

After the teams pick their character, they line up facing each other. Everyone starts out by saying Giants, Elves, Wizards and then the teams simultaneously act out their chosen characters. The winning characters chase the beaten characters, trying to tag them before they reach their safe zone. Anyone tagged switches to the other team.

Variations

  • Food Chain Variation: Use animals instead of mythical characters (i.e. Bear, Salmon, Mosquito)

Equipment

  • Markers for lines and boundaries (ropes, cones, etc.)

Home
  • Raccoon Circle Race TrackAdd

Guidelines

Standing in a circle with both hands on the webbing circle, the group ‘drives’ the knot around making racecar noises. Anyone can hit the brakes and make a screeching noise. The person closest to the knot shares their name and something about themselves.

Variations

  • Give the group a specific category for sharing (favorite food, goal for the day, etc.)
  • Participants share name, a word that starts with the same letter, and how that word is somehow symbolic of them
  • Participants share two things about their life history - one is true, one is false. The group guesses which is which.

Ode to the Chicken

Home
  • Ode to the ChickenAdd

Guidelines

Participants are in a circle and each has a carpet square or other place marker – minus one. The left over person is IT and is in the middle with the rubber chicken and its coop. IT tags a person in the circle (in an appropriate place and manner), puts the chicken on the coop, and tries to get to the tagged person’s spot before the tagged person picks up the chicken and tags them back. If the chicken falls off or misses the coop, everyone yells “fowl play!” After a few rounds, add that any unoccupied space can be occupied by anyone else – at risk of being tagged when off a spot. Last, add that putting your head as close to the coop as possible and yelling “ode to the chicken” as loud as possible and returning to a spot without being tagged is worthy of huge respect.

Variations

  • Run Around addition: If someone leaves their spot and makes it all the way around the rubber chicken and back to their own spot without getting tagged, everyone must jump up and down and yell “Run Around!!”
  • Play upbeat music while playing

Equipment

  • Rubber chicken, spots (1 per person, minus 1), chicken coop (backpack, milk crate, stack of spots, etc.)

Desired Learned Outcomes

  • Communication
  • Group Problem Solving

Chicken Towers

Home
  • Chicken TowersAdd

Guidelines

Split the larger group into groups of 3-5 and direct them to build the tallest free standing structure they can with a chicken on top. They have a few minutes to plan and a few minutes to build.

Variations

  • Give a specified planning time during which they cannot touch the pieces
  • Once they start building they can no longer talk
  • Create roles including who designs, who gives directions, and who builds

Equipment

  • Lots of PVC pipe pieces of varying lengths, a variety of pipe connectors, rubber chickens (1 per small group)

Desired Learned Outcomes

  • Communication
  • Group Problem Solving

Reflection Questions

  1. How much did the smaller groups communicate with and help each other?
  2. Where they competing or cooperating? Why?
  3. Where else in your life do you see this happening? When is it good? Bad?

Not A Knot

Home
  • Not A KnotAdd

Guidelines

Show the group a small section of rope that looks like it may turn into a knot when pulled from both sides (such as a daisy chain). Have the group try to decide if it will be a knot or not, without touching or moving the rope. Then have them divide themselves into sides depending on what they think. The group needs to come to an agreement before it is pulled out so they must convince each other. Once everyone is on the same side, pull the rope from both ends.

Equipment

  • Short piece of rope

Desired Learned Outcomes

  • Communication

Reflection Questions

  1. How did you get the whole group to come to an agreement?
  2. What forms of communication were successful?
  3. Where else in life do you have to make decisions as a group?

Marshmallow Pit

Home
  • Marshmallow PitAdd

Guidelines

Set a square boundary with rope, and fill the inside with spots. Split the group into 4 smaller groups to start at the 4 corners. Everyone must cross the pit (mug of hot chocolate perhaps) by stepping on the spots (marshmallows), but once the spots are stepped on they will disappear (sink) if no longer stepped on. If anyone steps in the hot chocolate, everyone must go back to their starting corners.

Variations

  • If someone steps in the pit, only that person goes back to their starting corner
  • The group can earn back lost marshmallows by making sacrifices (losing voices, sight, etc.)

Equipment

  • Boundary rope, plenty of spots (about 2 spots for every 3 people, give or take)

Desired Learned Outcomes

  • Communication
  • Group Problem Solving

Reflection Questions

  1. How much did the 4 smaller groups communicate with and help each other?
  2. Where they competing or cooperating? Why?
  3. Where else in your life do you see this happening? When is it good? Bad?

Agent Smith

Home
  • Agent SmithAdd

Guidelines

Everyone has a partner, one is blindfolded (Agent Smith). The non-blindfolded partner is the protector/director for their agent. Agents try to get other agents out by hitting them with soft throwable objects while their partners protect them and verbally direct them to more ammo on the ground. Protector/director partners cannot touch their agents or ammo on the ground.

Variations

  • Each tank has only one throwable object that they can use
  • Agents are not “out” if hit by an object, but rather deactivate on the spot. If they are hit by a stray throw, they are reactivated.

Equipment

  • Lots of soft throwable objects; blindfolds for half of the group

Desired Learned Outcomes

  • Trust Building
  • Communication

Reflection Questions

  1. Which role was more challenging? Why?
  2. What did your partner do that worked well for you?
  3. How did you know if you could trust your partner?

Comfort Zone Activity

Home
  • Comfort Zone ActivityAdd

Guidelines

Create a circle with webbing/rope/cones, then a larger one outside it. Discuss the comfort/challenge/danger zones (or whatever you call them). Call out various things and have people move to where they would be in the zones as a result.

Variations

  2. How does that affect our group?
  3. How should we take that into consideration as we work together?

Group Hop (Do As I Do)

Home
  • Group Hop (Do As I Do)Add

Guidelines

The group, including the facilitator, stands in a circle and links arms at the elbows. Round 1: the facilitator calls out directions of “Left,” “Right,” “Front,” or “Back,” and the group must call out same direction and then hop once in that direction together. Round 2: the group calls out same direction but hops in opposite direction. Round 3: the group calls out opposite direction but hops in same direction as called by the facilitator.

Variations

  • Facilitator stands in the middle rather than as a part of the circle.
  • Add another easier round where group calls out opposite and goes that way.
  • Advanced: Call out more than one direction at a time.

Desired Learned Outcomes

  • Group Problem Solving
  • Communication

Reflection Questions

  1. Was it easy or hard to follow the directions? Why?
  2. What did you do to be successful anyway?
  3. Where else do you see people say one thing and do the opposite?

Key Punch

Home
  • Key PunchAdd

Guidelines

Scatter 30 numbered spots inside of a designated area (rope or cones). The group must touch the numbered spots in order as fast as they can. The group starts behind a line that is well away from the circle, and time begins when someone crosses the line and stops when the last person crosses back over the line again. Only one person can be in the key punch area at a time. Everyone must touch a spot before anyone can touch another spot. If spots are touched out of sequence or two people enter area at once, 10 second penalties are assessed.

Variations

  • Repeat multiple times to focus on improving and goal setting.
  • Vault Code - Create a specific code to unlock the “vault” (not 1-30).
  • Secret Code: Make the group figure out the secret code.

Desired Learned Outcomes

  • Group Problem Solving
  • Communication

Equipment

  • 30 numbered spots, rope or cones for boundary, starting line

Reflection Questions

  1. What did your group do well together as a team?
  2. What was one key to this group being highly effective?
  3. What did you do as a group to get faster each time?

Hula Hoop Pass

Home
  • Hula Hoop PassAdd

Guidelines

Make a circle holding hands. Put a hula hoop on. Pass it around, using NO thumbs or fingers and without breaking the circle. Pass two around, in opposite directions. Large groups equal more hoops!

Variations

  • Use a webbing circle instead of a hula hoop
  • Use a bike tire or tube instead of a hula hoop
  • Use a raccoon circle tied into a figure eight. Each half of the eight goes around the circle opposite directions.

Desired Learned Outcomes

  • Group Support

Equipment

  • Hoops – the number and type to fit the group

Reflection Questions

  1. How did you support each other when things got tricky?
  2. How did it feel to have both hula hoops arrive at you at the same time?

Pipeline

Home
  • PipelineAdd

Guidelines

Each participant gets a piece of pipeline. The group must roll a ball down the entire pipeline from point A to point B - no stopping or rolling backwards. If the ball stops, rolls backwards, or is dropped, the group must start over.

Variations

  1. What were some effective forms of communication you used? Ineffective forms?
  2. How could we improve our communication to improve our problem solving skills?

Mousetraps

Home
  • MousetrapsAdd

Guidelines

Show everyone how to set a mousetrap properly. Set it off by dropping it on the ground. Distribute traps to those who want them and let them try. Reset the mousetraps and demonstrate how to set it off with your hands (firm hand sandwich). Allow those willing to try it for themselves. Next, have willing participants find a partner, with one mousetrap between them. One person will hold the set mousetrap and be the bottom hand of the sandwich, while the other person will be the top hand. Partners will need to come up with a plan and communicate about what they are doing.

Variations

  • Have one partner close their eyes while the other partner directs them.
  • Have group stand in a circle with a set mousetrap in everyone’s left hand. Together, everyone uses their right hand as the top of the hand sandwich for the person on their right.
  • Come up with names for the various part of the mousetrap with the group. This gives the group shared language to use and can add some silly fun.

Desired Learned Outcomes

  • Trust Building

Equipment

  • Mousetraps (1 per person)

Reflection Questions

  1. What did your partners do to encourage trust?
  2. Did that help you overcome your fear?
  3. How can we make sure we are encouraging others to trust us?

Trust Lean

Home
  • Trust LeanAdd

Guidelines

Have each person get a partner of similar size. Have one person agree to be the leaner first. The leaner position is to be stiff as a board with arms crossed over their chest and legs together. Do not bend at the waist! The other partner is the spotter and should be in proper spotting position and stance. Have the spotter stand behind the leaner, with hands on the leaner’s back, and get prepared to carefully lean them back. They will go through the spotter’s contract and then the leaning begins. Spotters should only lean their partner as far as they feel comfortable with. Switch roles.

Variations

  • Have leaners close their eyes
  • Do multiple leans, going farther back each time
  • Spotters start without their hands on the leaners’ backs so there is a bit of a “fall” first

Desired Learned Outcomes

  • Trust Building

Reflection Questions

  1. Was it more challenging to be the leaner or the spotter? Why?
  2. What did your partner do that you really appreciated?

Wind in the Willows

Home
  • Wind in the WillowsAdd

Guidelines

Participants should already know how to spot. Make a tight circle standing shoulder to shoulder. Make sure that the strength of the group is equally divided around the circle. Have a volunteer stand in the middle. The leader of the game asks everyone to stand in a line facing the leader. They are to follow the commands that are said. If they do a command when 'Order' is not said then they are out. The trick to the game is that they must watch the leader at all times and do what they SAY and not what they DO (so, the leader is trying to be tricky). The participants can only do the six actions. Once they are out they can help judge the others. The last one left is the winner of the universe.
